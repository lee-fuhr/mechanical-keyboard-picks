Frequently asked questions

Can a budget mechanical keyboard feel good?

Yes. The switch quality in budget keyboards has improved dramatically. The Royal Kludge RK84 uses Gateron-equivalent switches that type well. The main sacrifices are case build quality, keycap durability, and software, not the core typing experience.

What do you give up with a budget mechanical keyboard?

Usually: premium case materials (plastic vs aluminum), durable double-shot PBT keycaps (cheaper boards use thin ABS), more sophisticated software, and longer warranty. The switch feel is often surprisingly close to pricier options.

Is a $40 mechanical keyboard worth it?

For a first mechanical keyboard or someone trying out the format, yes. You can experience hot-swap, try different switches, and assess whether you prefer linear or tactile before investing in a $100-150 board.

Are budget keyboards compatible with aftermarket keycaps?

Most use standard Cherry MX-compatible layouts, so most aftermarket keycap sets fit. Check that the layout matches, some budget boards have non-standard bottom rows that won't accept standard replacement spacebar sets.

Do budget mechanical keyboards break quickly?

The switches themselves are durable (50+ million keystrokes). The parts that degrade on budget boards are ABS keycaps (shine over time), USB cables (feel cheap), and stabilizers (can rattle). The core typing function holds up well.
